# Paul Graham Essay Traits

Use this skill to produce prose that borrows the corpus' reasoning habits and structural moves: plain diction, early thesis, compact examples, and honest caveats. Do not aim for impersonation; aim for adjacent strengths.

## Workflow

1. Define the animating question.
- Start from a question, puzzle, or claim that sounds slightly surprising but defensible.
- State the answer early, usually in the first one to three paragraphs.
- Prefer a simple title: a noun phrase, a short command, or a direct question.

2. Build the argument spine before filling in prose.
- Reduce the piece to a sequence of claims.
- Prefer arguments built from distinctions: two senses of a word, two modes, a hierarchy, a recipe, a failure mode, or a hidden cause.
- Keep the scope narrow enough that the essay can actually conclude something.

3. Develop claims with examples and analogies.
- Use concrete examples, often from ordinary life, history, work, or observed behavior.
- Use analogies to compress reasoning, not to decorate.
- Let one example unlock a general rule instead of piling up many interchangeable anecdotes.

4. Keep the voice plain and conversational.
- Prefer simple, mostly germanic words.
- Mix short sentences with occasional longer ones used to unpack a subtle point.
- Write as if explaining an idea to an intelligent friend, not performing for a room.
- Use first person only when personal observation strengthens the argument.

5. Revise for compression and rhythm.
- Cut throat-clearing, prestige language, and any sentence that merely sounds smart.
- Make each paragraph do one job.
- Preserve momentum by ending paragraphs on a forward-driving sentence.
- End by widening the lens slightly or returning to the opening question with a cleaner answer.

## Guardrails

- Do not imitate Paul Graham verbatim.
- Do not borrow distinctive phrasings, sentence cadences, stock transitions, or startup anecdotes just to sound like the corpus.
- Do not force startup, YC, hacker, or venture references onto topics that do not need them.
- Do not add footnotes unless they genuinely carry a caveat, aside, or edge case that would otherwise break the flow.
- Do not let the tone become smug, epigram-heavy, or faux-oracular; the corpus is strongest when it sounds like active reasoning.
- Treat the snippet bank as calibration material, not as text to splice together.

## Output Shapes

- Full essay: use a simple heading and a clean body of connected paragraphs; add notes only when they help.
- Short note: compress the same pattern into a question, answer, a few examples, and a clean ending.
- Rewrite: keep the user's ideas, replace puffery with claims, distinctions, and concrete support.
