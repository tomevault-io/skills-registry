# Feel-Felt-Found (3 F's) Technique

## Overview

The Feel-Felt-Found technique is a classic sales objection handling method that uses emotional validation and social proof to address prospect concerns. It's particularly effective for objections rooted in emotions, uncertainty, or risk aversion rather than factual concerns.

The technique works by:
1. Validating the prospect's feelings
2. Normalizing their concern through social proof
3. Sharing positive outcomes from similar customers

This approach builds trust by showing empathy while demonstrating that others in similar situations have succeeded.

## When to Use

- Objections that are emotionally-driven (starting with "I think" or "I feel")
- Risk-aversion concerns ("What if it doesn't work?")
- Uncertainty about change ("We've always done it this way")
- Price objections based on perceived value vs. actual ROI
- First-time buyer hesitation
- When you have strong customer success stories from similar situations

## When NOT to Use

- Objections requiring factual or technical information
- Compliance or regulatory concerns (need specific answers)
- Budget constraints that are genuine (not perceived value issues)
- When overused repeatedly in the same conversation (becomes formulaic)
- If you don't have relevant customer examples (would sound insincere)

## Core Framework

### The 3 F's Structure

**1. Feel**: Validate their emotion

Acknowledge and validate the prospect's feeling without agreeing with their conclusion. This shows empathy and builds rapport.

**Template**: "I understand how you feel about [concern]"

**2. Felt**: Normalize with social proof

Share that others (specifically customers) have felt the same way. This normalizes their concern and shows they're not alone.

**Template**: "Other customers felt the same way" or "Many of our current customers felt exactly the same before [implementation/purchase]"

**3. Found**: Share the positive outcome

Describe what those customers discovered or experienced after moving forward. This provides social proof and a vision of success.

**Template**: "What they found was [positive outcome with specifics]"

## Talk Tracks

### Price Objection (Perceived Investment Concern)

**Objection**: "This seems expensive" or "The price is higher than I expected"

**Response**:
> "I understand how you feel; this solution may seem like a significant investment upfront. Many of our current customers felt exactly the same way before implementation. What they found was that the ROI far exceeded their expectations, with most seeing payback within 90 days. For example, [Company X] in your industry had the same concern, and they're now saving $50K per quarter."

### Risk/Uncertainty Objection

**Objection**: "What if this doesn't work for us?" or "We're not sure if this will solve our problem"

**Response**:
> "I completely understand how you feel about taking this step—it's a significant decision. Other companies in [industry/situation] felt the exact same uncertainty before getting started. What they found was that our onboarding process and support team made the transition smoother than expected, and they saw results within the first 30 days. Would it help if I connected you with [similar customer] to hear their experience directly?"

### Change Management Objection

**Objection**: "Our team might resist this change" or "We've always done it the old way"

**Response**:
> "I hear you—change management is always a concern, and I understand how you feel about potential resistance. Other teams felt the same way about shifting from their existing process. What they found was that once users saw the time savings in the first week, adoption accelerated quickly. One customer told us their biggest skeptic became their internal champion within a month."

### Timing Objection (Emotional)

**Objection**: "I'm not sure if now is the right time"

**Response**:
> "I understand how you feel—timing is important, and there's never a perfect moment for change. Other customers felt that same hesitation about timing. What they found was that the longer they waited, the more they realized they were losing by not acting. Companies that started mid-year actually found it easier than those who waited for a 'perfect' quarter."

### Comparison Objection

**Objection**: "We're looking at other solutions too"

**Response**:
> "I completely understand how you feel—with so many options, it makes sense to evaluate alternatives. Many of our customers felt they should compare multiple vendors before deciding. What they found was that while other solutions looked similar on paper, our [specific differentiator] made a significant difference in their results. Would it be helpful if I showed you what made [Customer X] choose us over [Competitor]?"

## Data-Driven Insights

- **Top performers ask questions 54.3% of the time when responding to objections** vs. 31% for average reps (Gong Labs)
  - After using Feel-Felt-Found, follow up with a question rather than continuing to talk
  - Example: "What they found was [outcome]. What concerns does that address for you?"

- **Social proof is most effective when specific** (company name, industry, metrics) rather than generic ("many customers")

- **Emotional validation precedes logical justification** - people buy emotionally and justify logically (Sandler)

## Quick Reference

### Structure at a Glance

1. **Feel**: "I understand how you feel about [concern]"
2. **Felt**: "Other customers/companies felt the same way"
3. **Found**: "What they found was [positive outcome]"

### Key Success Factors

✅ **Make it genuine** - Don't use a robotic formula; adapt the language naturally
✅ **Be specific** - Name actual customers (with permission) or specific outcomes
✅ **Match the emotion** - Mirror their concern before flipping to positive outcome
✅ **Follow with a question** - Don't just talk; engage after the technique
✅ **Have real examples ready** - The "Found" must be credible and relevant

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Using the exact same phrasing every time (sounds scripted)
❌ Generic social proof ("everyone loves it")
❌ Skipping straight to "Found" without validating feelings
❌ Using it for objections that need factual answers
❌ Overusing in a single conversation

### When to Pivot to Another Technique

- If objection is factual/technical → Use `laer-framework` + specific answer
- If multiple objections → Use `isolate-and-address`
- If pricing needs ROI justification → Use `value-selling-roi`
- If deep emotional exploration needed → Use `sandler-pain-funnel`

## Additional Tips

### Building Your "Felt-Found" Library

Maintain a collection of real customer stories organized by:
- **Objection type** (price, timing, risk, change management)
- **Industry** (so you can match to similar prospects)
- **Deal size** (small business vs. enterprise concerns differ)
- **Outcome metrics** (specific results to reference)

### Making It Conversational

Instead of the formulaic phrasing, adapt to sound natural:
- Feel: "I totally get that" / "That makes sense" / "I hear you"
- Felt: "You're not alone in that" / "We hear that a lot from [X]"
- Found: "Here's what's interesting..." / "The surprising thing was..."

The principle matters more than the exact words.

### Strengthening the "Found" Component

Make outcomes specific and measurable:
- ❌ "They were happy with the results"
- ✅ "They reduced processing time from 4 hours to 45 minutes"

Include emotional outcomes too:
- "Their team went from skeptical to asking for more licenses"
- "The CFO called it the best investment they made that quarter"

## Examples by Scenario

### SaaS Software Purchase
**Objection**: "Our team is already overwhelmed with tools"

**Response**: "I completely understand how you feel—adding another platform can seem like more complexity. Teams at [Company A] and [Company B] felt the exact same way before consolidating with us. What they found was that our solution actually replaced 3 separate tools they were using, and their team saved 10 hours per week. Their ops manager told me the hardest part was deciding which old tools to sunset first."

### Consulting Services
**Objection**: "We've tried consultants before and didn't see results"

**Response**: "I hear you—that's frustrating, and I understand how you feel about investing again after a disappointing experience. Several of our current clients felt burned by previous consulting engagements before working with us. What they found was that our hands-on implementation approach (not just recommendations) made the difference. [Client X] had a similar experience with [Previous Firm] and saw measurable results within 60 days with us versus months of stalled reports before."

### Enterprise Implementation
**Objection**: "The implementation seems too disruptive"

**Response**: "I understand how you feel about disruption—no one wants to slow down current operations. Companies like [Company C] felt the same concern about rolling this out across 500 users. What they found was that our phased implementation approach let them start with one department, prove value, then expand—so there was never a 'big bang' disruption. They actually accelerated the timeline because early adopters were asking when their teams could get access."
