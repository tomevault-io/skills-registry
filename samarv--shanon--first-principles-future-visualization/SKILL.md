This framework allows product leaders to bypass current technical constraints and "induced demand" by visualizing the world as it will inevitably exist based on fundamental physics and economics.

## The "Mountain Finding" Workflow

### 1. Defrag the Calendar
Deep strategic visualization cannot happen in 30-minute increments between OKR reviews.
- Carve out a minimum of 2-4 hours of uninterrupted "deep work" time.
- Physically remove yourself from the office or digital distractions.
- Engage in "low-input" activities (hiking, running, or a silent commute) to allow the brain to move into a contemplative state.

### 2. Identify First Principles
Strip the problem down to its core physics and economic realities. Question the "why" behind every current industry standard.
- **Example Question:** "Why do we need a 4,000-pound vehicle to move one 150-pound human three miles?"
- **Analysis:** Identify if the current solution is fundamentally inefficient. If the physics don't make sense, a massive opportunity for a "mountain" exists (e.g., micro-mobility, autonomous pods).

### 3. The 10-Year Visualization Exercise
Close your eyes and build a salient, bird’s-eye view of the future 10 years from now.
- **Ignore your company's role initially:** Visualize how the world moves and operates absent of your product.
- **Look for the "Given" trends:** Identify technologies that are inevitable (e.g., "Autonomy is a given," "Costs of sensors will trend to zero").
- **Predict second-order effects:** If X becomes cheap and autonomous, what happens to Y? (e.g., If cars are autonomous and cheap, do parking garages become parks? Does traffic increase due to "induced demand"?).

### 4. Apply the "Mountain" Lens
Once the future landscape is clear, determine your product’s relationship to it:
- **Tailwinds:** How can the product ride the inevitable trends?
- **Friction:** Where does the current product rub against the future reality?
- **Intervention:** What specific piece of this future can only your team manifest?

### 5. Transition to the Brain Trust
Move from solo visualization to co-creation to stress-test the vision.
- **The "Whiteboard Only" Rule:** Gather 5-8 key stakeholders (Eng, Design, Data) for a full day.
- **Laptops Down:** Focus entirely on the whiteboard.
- **"Yes, And" Culture:** Use the Pixar "Brain Trust" model—riff on ideas without judgment or attachment to being "right."

## Examples

**Example 1: The Future of Urban Mobility**
- **Context:** A PM at a ride-sharing company looking for the next 5-year arc.
- **First Principle:** Moving people in cities is currently high-mass and high-energy.
- **Visualization:** 10 years out, autonomy makes single-occupancy vehicles so cheap that streets are perpetually gridlocked (induced demand).
- **Strategy:** Shared rides (pooling) and multi-modality (bikes/scooters) are not just "options" but physical necessities for a functioning city.
- **Output:** Shift investment from "cheaper single rides" to "high-capacity autonomous pooling infrastructure."

**Example 2: Marketplace Logistics**
- **Context:** Transitioning from a simple single-product marketplace to a complex multi-modal one.
- **Visualization:** A world where supply is no longer just "independent contractors" but includes autonomous fleets, third-party taxi companies, and different vehicle form factors.
- **Strategy:** Build a "Logistics Brain" that is supply-agnostic and optimizes for the highest utilization across all possible modes.
- **Output:** A multi-year tech roadmap for a marketplace that prices and matches based on supply type and quality rather than just distance.

## Common Pitfalls

- **The "Theory Land" Trap:** Spending weeks on beautiful whiteboard diagrams and PhD-level algorithms without building a prototype. If engineers describe the vision as "boiling the ocean," you have tilted too far into vision and need to pivot to execution.
- **"Ready, Fire, Aim":** Running through walls without checking if it's the right wall. This happens when teams skip the "First Principles" step and simply execute faster on a flawed premise.
- **The Content Addiction:** Filling every gap in the day with podcasts or music. True strategy requires "mind like water"—emptying the mind of to-dos so that creative energy can birth new ideas.
- **Mistaking the Finger for the Moon:** Focusing on the "optics" (the deck, the OKR presentation) rather than the actual product outcome. The presentation is a means to an end; the "mountain" is the actual impact on the world.
